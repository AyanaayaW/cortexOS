# 2.3 Macroeconomic Objectives

## 1. Low and Stable Unemployment

### Definitions
- **Unemployment**: People of working age who are without work, available for work, and actively seeking employment.
- **Labor Force**: The employed + the unemployed (Does not include students, retirees, discouraged workers).
- **Full Employment**: The level of employment where there is no cyclical unemployment (though frictional, structural, and seasonal unemployment still exist). This is the Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU).

### Formulas
- **Unemployment Rate = (Number of Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100**

### Types of Unemployment
1. **Frictional Unemployment**: Short-term unemployment that occurs when people are in between jobs or entering the labor market (e.g., recent graduates).
2. **Structural Unemployment**: Long-term unemployment caused by a mismatch between the skills workers possess and the skills demanded by employers (due to technological changes, globalization, or changes in consumer demand). *Most serious type.*
3. **Seasonal Unemployment**: Unemployment linked to seasonal demand for labor (e.g., ski instructors, agriculture).
4. **Cyclical (Demand-deficient) Unemployment**: Unemployment resulting from a fall in Aggregate Demand during the contraction phase of the business cycle.

## 2. Low and Stable Rate of Inflation

### Definitions
- **Inflation**: A sustained increase in the general/average price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. (Money loses purchasing power).
- **Deflation**: A sustained decrease in the general/average price level (Inflation rate is negative).
- **Disinflation**: A decrease in the rate of inflation (prices are still rising, but at a slower pace. e.g., from 5% to 2%).
- **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**: A measure of the cost of living for the typical household. It tracks changes in the price of a fixed "basket" of goods and services commonly purchased by consumers.

### Formulas
- **CPI = (Cost of basket in current year / Cost of basket in base year) × 100**
- **Inflation Rate = ((CPI Year 2 - CPI Year 1) / CPI Year 1) × 100**

### Types/Causes of Inflation
1. **Demand-Pull Inflation**: Caused by an increase in Aggregate Demand (AD shifts right) when the economy is close to or at full employment. "Too much money chasing too few goods."
2. **Cost-Push Inflation**: Caused by a decrease in Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS shifts left) due to rising costs of production (e.g., higher wages, oil price shocks). Leads to **Stagflation** (stagnant growth + inflation).

### Consequences of High Inflation
- Decreases purchasing power.
- Harms savers (if inflation rate > interest rate).
- Benefits borrowers (real value of debt falls).
- Reduces international competitiveness (exports become more expensive).
- Creates uncertainty, reducing investment.

## 3. Economic Growth

### Definitions
- **Economic Growth**: An increase in real GDP over time.
- **Short-Run Growth**: An increase in actual output resulting from utilizing unemployed resources (Shift of AD or SRAS; moving towards the PPF curve).
- **Long-Run Growth**: An increase in the potential output/productive capacity of the economy (Shift of LRAS; outward shift of the PPF). Caused by an increase in the *quantity* or *quality* of factors of production.

## 4. Equity in the Distribution of Income

### Definitions
- **Equity**: Fairness (not necessarily equality). Ensuring everyone has a fair standard of living and equal opportunities.
- **Equality**: Everyone receives the exact same income.

### Measurement
- **Lorenz Curve**: A visual representation of income inequality. The further the curve sags away from the Line of Absolute Equality, the greater the inequality.

- **Gini Coefficient**: A statistical measure of inequality derived from the Lorenz Curve.
  - **Formula: Area A / (Area A + Area B)**
  - Value ranges from 0 to 1 (or 0 to 100).
  - 0 = Perfect equality (everyone has the exact same income).
  - 1 = Perfect inequality (one person has all the income).

### Government Redistribution Policies (Taxes)
1. **Progressive Tax**: As income increases, the *percentage* paid in tax increases (e.g., income tax brackets). Best for reducing inequality.
2. **Regressive Tax**: As income increases, the *percentage* paid in tax decreases (e.g., sales taxes/VAT, as they take up a larger proportion of a poor person's income).
3. **Proportional (Flat) Tax**: The percentage of tax paid remains constant, regardless of income.
