# 2.2 Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply (AD-AS Model)

## Aggregate Demand (AD)

### Definitions
- **Aggregate Demand (AD)**: The total quantity of all goods and services (real GDP) demanded in an economy at different average price levels, over a specific time period.
- **Formula**: **AD = C + I + G + (X - M)**

### Why is the AD curve downward sloping?
1. **The Wealth Effect**: If the average price level increases, the real value of consumers' wealth/savings falls, leading them to buy less (C falls).
2. **The Interest Rate Effect**: A higher price level means consumers/firms need more money for transactions. This increased demand for money drives up interest rates, which discourages borrowing and reduces Investment (I) and Consumption (C).
3. **The Net Export Effect**: If domestic price levels rise relative to other countries, exports become less competitive (X falls) and imports become relatively cheaper (M rises). Thus, net exports fall.

### Determinants of AD (Shifts in the AD Curve)
Any change in the components of AD will shift the curve:
1. **Consumption (C)**: Changes in consumer confidence, interest rates, wealth, personal income taxes, level of household debt.
2. **Investment (I)**: Changes in business confidence, interest rates, corporate taxes, level of corporate debt, technology.
3. **Government Spending (G)**: Political and economic priorities (e.g., building infrastructure, military spending).
4. **Net Exports (X-M)**: Changes in the incomes of trading partners, exchange rates, trade policies.

## Aggregate Supply (AS)

### Definitions
- **Aggregate Supply (AS)**: The total quantity of all goods and services (real GDP) produced in an economy at different average price levels, over a specific time period.

### Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)
- **Short-Run**: The period of time where the prices of factors of production (especially wages) are relatively inflexible or "sticky."
- The SRAS curve is upward sloping because as the price level increases (while wages are fixed), profitability increases, incentivizing firms to produce more.
- **Determinants of SRAS (Shifts)**:
  1. Changes in resource prices (e.g., wages, raw materials, oil).
  2. Changes in business taxes or subsidies.
  3. Supply shocks (e.g., natural disasters destroying infrastructure).

### Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)
- **Long-Run**: The period of time where all prices of factors of production (including wages) are fully flexible and adapt to changes in the price level.
- Represents the **Potential Output (Yfe)** or Full Employment level of output of an economy.

#### Two Views of LRAS:
1. **Monetarist/New Classical View**:
   - The LRAS curve is perfectly vertical at the full employment level of output (Yfe).
   - They believe the economy is self-correcting in the long run. Any change in AD will only affect the price level in the long run, not real GDP.
2. **Keynesian View**:
   - The AS curve has three sections:
     - *Horizontal section*: Deep recession, lots of spare capacity (high unemployment). Output can increase without driving up prices.
     - *Upward sloping section*: Economy approaches full capacity, bottlenecks occur, pushing prices up.
     - *Vertical section*: Absolute full capacity. Cannot increase output further, only prices rise.
   - Keynesians argue that wages are "sticky downwards" (due to labor unions, minimum wages, contracts), meaning the economy can get stuck in a recessionary gap indefinitely without government intervention.

## Macroeconomic Equilibrium
Occurs where AD intersects AS.
- **Recessionary (Deflationary) Gap**: When equilibrium Real GDP is less than Potential GDP (Y < Yfe). High unemployment.
- **Inflationary Gap**: When equilibrium Real GDP is greater than Potential GDP (Y > Yfe). Demand outpaces capacity, causing inflation.
