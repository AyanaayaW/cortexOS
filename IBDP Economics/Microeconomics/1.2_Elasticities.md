# 1.2 Elasticities

## Definitions
- **Elasticity**: A measure of the responsiveness of one variable to a change in another variable.
- **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**: A measure of how much the quantity demanded of a good responds to a change in its price.
- **Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)**: A measure of the responsiveness of the quantity demanded of one good to a change in the price of another good.
- **Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)**: A measure of the responsiveness of the quantity demanded of a good to a change in consumer income.
- **Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)**: A measure of the responsiveness of the quantity supplied of a good to a change in its price.

---

## Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

### Formula
**PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP**
*(Percentage change in Quantity Demanded divided by Percentage change in Price)*

**Where %Δ = ((New - Old) / Old) × 100**

*(Note: PED is always negative due to the law of demand, but it is typically expressed as an absolute value |PED|).*

### Values and Interpretation
- **|PED| > 1**: Elastic demand. Consumers are highly responsive to price changes (flatter curve).
- **|PED| < 1**: Inelastic demand. Consumers are largely unresponsive to price changes (steeper curve).
- **|PED| = 1**: Unitary elastic demand. A % change in price leads to an equal % change in Qd.
- **|PED| = 0**: Perfectly inelastic demand. Quantity demanded does not change regardless of price (vertical line).
- **|PED| = ∞**: Perfectly elastic demand. Consumers will buy an infinite amount at a specific price, but none at any higher price (horizontal line).

### Determinants of PED (SPLAT)
1. **S**ubstitutes: Number and closeness of substitutes (More substitutes = more elastic).
2. **P**roportion of Income: Goods that take up a large portion of income are more elastic.
3. **L**uxury vs. Necessity: Luxuries are elastic; necessities are inelastic.
4. **A**ddictive/Habit-forming nature: Addictive goods are highly inelastic (e.g., cigarettes).
5. **T**ime: Demand becomes more elastic over time as consumers find alternatives.

---

## Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)

### Formula
**XED = %ΔQd of Good A / %ΔPrice of Good B**

### Values and Interpretation
- **XED > 0 (Positive)**: The goods are **Substitutes**. (e.g., If price of Coke increases, Qd of Pepsi increases). A higher absolute value means they are closer substitutes.
- **XED < 0 (Negative)**: The goods are **Complements**. (e.g., If price of printers increases, Qd of ink decreases). A larger negative value means they are stronger complements.
- **XED = 0**: The goods are **Unrelated** (e.g., cars and apples).

---

## Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

### Formula
**YED = %ΔQd / %ΔIncome (Y)**

### Values and Interpretation
- **YED > 0 (Positive)**: **Normal Good**. As income rises, demand rises.
  - **0 < YED < 1**: Income-inelastic (Necessities like basic food).
  - **YED > 1**: Income-elastic (Luxuries like sports cars, fine dining).
- **YED < 0 (Negative)**: **Inferior Good**. As income rises, demand falls (e.g., generic brands, instant noodles).

---

## Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

### Formula
**PES = %ΔQs / %ΔP**

### Values and Interpretation
- **PES > 1**: Elastic supply. Producers can quickly and easily increase output in response to a price increase.
- **PES < 1**: Inelastic supply. Producers are unable to quickly increase output (e.g., agricultural products that take time to grow).
- **PES = 1**: Unitary elastic supply (Supply curve passes through the origin).
- **PES = 0**: Perfectly inelastic supply. Quantity supplied is fixed regardless of price (e.g., tickets to a stadium with fixed seating).

### Determinants of PES
1. **Time**: PES is highly inelastic in the short run and becomes more elastic in the long run as firms can expand capacity.
2. **Mobility of Factors of Production**: If resources can be easily shifted from producing one good to another, PES is high.
3. **Unused Capacity**: If a firm is operating below full capacity, it can easily increase output (Elastic). If at full capacity, it cannot (Inelastic).
4. **Ability to Store Stock**: If goods can be stored cheaply (non-perishable), firms can react quickly to price changes by releasing inventory (Elastic).
