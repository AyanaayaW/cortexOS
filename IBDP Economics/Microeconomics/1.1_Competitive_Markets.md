# 1.1 Competitive Markets: Demand and Supply

## Definitions
- **Market**: A place or mechanism where buyers and sellers meet to exchange goods or services.
- **Demand**: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at various price levels, over a given period of time, *ceteris paribus*.
- **Law of Demand**: As the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa, *ceteris paribus*. (Negative/inverse relationship).
- **Ceteris Paribus**: A Latin phrase meaning "all other things being equal." Used to isolate the effect of one variable on another.
- **Normal Good**: A good for which demand increases as consumer income increases.
- **Inferior Good**: A good for which demand decreases as consumer income increases (e.g., instant noodles, generic brands).
- **Substitute Goods**: Goods that can be used in place of one another (e.g., Coke and Pepsi). An increase in the price of one leads to an increase in demand for the other.
- **Complementary Goods**: Goods that are typically consumed together (e.g., printers and ink cartridges). An increase in the price of one leads to a decrease in demand for the other.
- **Supply**: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various price levels, over a given period of time, *ceteris paribus*.
- **Law of Supply**: As the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa, *ceteris paribus*. (Positive/direct relationship).
- **Market Equilibrium**: The state where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied (Qd = Qs), meaning there is no tendency for the price to change.
- **Excess Demand (Shortage)**: When quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied (occurs when price is below equilibrium).
- **Excess Supply (Surplus)**: When quantity supplied is greater than quantity demanded (occurs when price is above equilibrium).

## Formulas & Functions

### Linear Demand Function
**Qd = a - bP**
- `Qd`: Quantity demanded
- `a`: The x-intercept (quantity demanded when price is 0). Influenced by non-price determinants of demand.
- `-b`: The slope/steepness of the demand curve. How responsive Qd is to a change in price (ΔQd / ΔP).
- `P`: Price

### Linear Supply Function
**Qs = c + dP**
- `Qs`: Quantity supplied
- `c`: The x-intercept (quantity supplied when price is 0). Influenced by non-price determinants of supply. If c is negative, it implies a minimum price must be reached before supply begins.
- `d`: The slope/steepness of the supply curve. How responsive Qs is to a change in price (ΔQs / ΔP).
- `P`: Price

### Calculating Market Equilibrium
To find the equilibrium price and quantity, set the demand and supply equations equal to each other:
**Qd = Qs**
(a - bP = c + dP)
Solve for `P` (Equilibrium Price), then plug `P` back into either equation to find `Q` (Equilibrium Quantity).

## Non-Price Determinants of Demand (Shifts the curve)
1. **Income** (Normal vs. Inferior goods)
2. **Preferences and Tastes** (Trends, advertising, seasonal changes)
3. **Prices of Related Goods** (Substitutes and Complements)
4. **Demographic changes** (Population size and composition)
5. **Future Price Expectations** (If consumers expect price to rise tomorrow, demand increases today)

## Non-Price Determinants of Supply (Shifts the curve)
1. **Costs of Factors of Production** (Wages, raw materials, rent)
2. **Technology** (Improvements increase supply by lowering average costs)
3. **Government Intervention** (Indirect taxes decrease supply; Subsidies increase supply)
4. **Prices of Related Goods** (Competitive supply vs. Joint supply)
5. **Expectations of Future Prices** (Producers might withhold supply now to sell later at a higher price)
6. **Number of Firms in the Market**
7. **Supply Shocks** (Weather, natural disasters, wars)
