# 1.3 Government Intervention & Market Failure

## Government Intervention

### Definitions
- **Indirect Tax**: A tax levied on goods and services, collected by the producer/seller on behalf of the government (e.g., VAT, Excise duty). It shifts the supply curve upwards by the amount of the tax.
- **Specific Tax**: A fixed amount of tax per unit sold (e.g., $2 per pack of cigarettes). Parallel shift of supply curve.
- **Ad Valorem Tax**: A tax that is a percentage of the selling price (e.g., 20% VAT). Divergent shift of the supply curve.
- **Subsidy**: A payment made by the government to a firm for each unit of output produced, aiming to lower production costs and increase supply.
- **Price Ceiling (Maximum Price)**: A legal maximum price set by the government, below the equilibrium price, to protect consumers (e.g., rent control). Leads to a shortage.
- **Price Floor (Minimum Price)**: A legal minimum price set by the government, above the equilibrium price, to protect producers or workers (e.g., minimum wage, agricultural price supports). Leads to a surplus.

### Tax Incidence (Burden of Tax)
The burden of an indirect tax depends on PED and PES:
- If **PED is inelastic** (PED < PES): Consumers bear a larger burden of the tax.
- If **PED is elastic** (PED > PES): Producers bear a larger burden of the tax.

## Market Failure

### Definitions
- **Market Failure**: A situation where the free market, left to its own devices, fails to allocate resources efficiently (allocative inefficiency), leading to an over-provision or under-provision of a good. (Marginal Social Benefit ≠ Marginal Social Cost).
- **Externality**: A spillover cost or benefit that falls on a third party who is not directly involved in the transaction (consumption or production) of a good.
- **Marginal Private Cost (MPC)**: The cost borne by the producer for producing one extra unit.
- **Marginal Social Cost (MSC)**: The total cost to society of producing one extra unit (MPC + External Costs).
- **Marginal Private Benefit (MPB)**: The benefit derived by the consumer from consuming one extra unit.
- **Marginal Social Benefit (MSB)**: The total benefit to society from consuming one extra unit (MPB + External Benefits).
- **Allocative Efficiency**: Occurs where MSC = MSB. The socially optimal level of output.

### Types of Externalities
1. **Negative Externality of Production (MSC > MPC)**
   - E.g., Factory pollution. The free market over-allocates resources (Q_market > Q_socially_optimum).
   - *Solutions*: Carbon taxes, tradable emission permits (cap and trade), regulations.

2. **Negative Externality of Consumption (MSB < MPB)**
   - E.g., Smoking, alcohol, junk food (Demerit goods). The free market over-allocates resources.
   - *Solutions*: Indirect taxes (Pigouvian tax), negative advertising, age restrictions.
3. **Positive Externality of Production (MSC < MPC)**
   - E.g., Firm investing in R&D or employee training that benefits other firms. The free market under-allocates resources.
   - *Solutions*: Direct government provision, subsidies.
4. **Positive Externality of Consumption (MSB > MPB)**
   - E.g., Education, healthcare, vaccinations (Merit goods). The free market under-allocates resources.
   - *Solutions*: Subsidies, positive advertising, legislation (mandatory vaccines).

### Public Goods vs. Private Goods
- **Private Goods**: Rivalrous (consumption by one prevents consumption by another) and Excludable (you can prevent non-payers from using it).
- **Public Goods**: Non-rivalrous and Non-excludable (e.g., national defense, street lighting, lighthouses).
  - *The Free Rider Problem*: Because public goods are non-excludable, individuals have no incentive to pay for them, hoping others will. Thus, the free market will not provide them (a complete market failure).

### Common Access Resources and Sustainability
- **Common Access Resources**: Goods that are non-excludable but rivalrous (e.g., fishing grounds, forests, clean air).
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: Because no one owns the resource, individuals act in their own self-interest and over-consume/deplete the resource, leading to environmental degradation.
- *Solutions*: Privatization (assigning property rights), quotas/licenses, carbon taxes.

### Asymmetric Information
A market failure where one party in a transaction possesses more or better information than the other (e.g., used car sales - "lemons", health insurance).
- **Adverse Selection**: Occurs before the transaction (e.g., only sick people buy health insurance).
- **Moral Hazard**: Occurs after the transaction (e.g., people driving recklessly because they are fully insured).
